# Warpedia — Charter

*The anchor document. What warpedia is, what it is not, and the principles all other decisions must align to.*

## What warpedia is

Warpedia is an **open, primary-source-rich, encyclopedic resource for the human record of war** — battles, conflicts, equipment, units, places, and the people (military and civilian) who lived through them.

It is built on the same foundational principles as Wikipedia and the broader Wikimedia ecosystem: open licensing, free access, neutral point of view, verifiability, and community-driven curation. Where Wikipedia provides encyclopedic prose with citations, warpedia adds two things Wikipedia structurally cannot:

1. **Primary sources hosted directly** — diaries, letters, photographs, oral histories, manuals, after-action reports, propaganda, war art — preserved alongside the encyclopedic content rather than only cited.
2. **Domain-specific structure** — entities and facets tuned to war (Equipment, Unit, Camp, Cemetery, Theme, Trial, Treaty) that Wikipedia's general-purpose article model cannot represent natively.

## What warpedia is not

- **Not a genealogy platform.** Ancestry, Fold3, FamilySearch, Findmypast serve people building family trees. Warpedia is not built around the "find my ancestor" workflow. Personal narratives are included as primary sources for the historical record, not as hooks for descendants.
- **Not a memorial / tributes site.** Find a Grave, Ever Loved, and the Vietnam Wall serve commemoration. Warpedia includes commemorative content (cemeteries, memorials, rolls of honour) as part of the historical record, but is not a place to "post a tribute".
- **Not an opinion forum.** No editorials, no comment sections in v1, no personal commentary on the items themselves. The encyclopedic posture demands this absence.
- **Not a defence-industry / current-operations resource.** Nothing operational, nothing classified, nothing on active conflicts in real time beyond what reputable secondary sources have published.
- **Not a competitor to Wikipedia.** Warpedia is the war-specific complement — covering what Wikipedia cannot (primary sources, sub-notability entities, war-specific structure) and aligning with Wikimedia as the long-term sustainability hedge.

## Core principles

1. **Open licensing.** All warpedia-original content (encyclopedic articles, curatorial themes, contributed primary sources) is published under CC BY-SA 4.0 or CC0. Mirroring with Wikipedia, Wikimedia Commons, and Wikisource is the long-term sustainability path.
2. **Primary sources are first-class.** A letter or diary entry is not "supporting evidence for an article" — it is a published item in its own right, with its own metadata, rights statement, and permanent identifier.
3. **Neutral point of view.** Encyclopedic content presents what is known, not what the author feels. Disputed interpretations are presented with attributed alternatives. Sources are cited.
4. **Verifiability over truth.** Every encyclopedic claim is traceable to a citation. Where the historical record is unclear, say so.
5. **Multi-conflict, multi-national, multi-perspective by design.** Warpedia covers all conflicts equally — not just WW1/WW2, not just Western/Anglophone, not just military. Civilian, colonial, women's, indigenous, and minority experiences are first-class concerns.
6. **AI-transparent.** AI-assisted enrichments (transcription, translation, summarization) are flagged, attributed to the model that produced them, and clearly distinguished from human-authored or human-reviewed content.
7. **Federated, not enclosed.** Where another open archive holds the canonical version of an item, warpedia links to it (with metadata mirrored locally) rather than re-hosting redundantly. Where rights permit, warpedia donates content upstream to Wikimedia Commons / Wikisource / Wikidata.
8. **Sustained for the long term.** Designed as a multi-decade open knowledge project, not a startup. Governance, infrastructure, and content storage chosen for durability that outlives any individual contributor.

## Position relative to existing players

| Resource | Their role | Warpedia's relationship |
|---|---|---|
| **Wikipedia** | Encyclopedic prose, general-purpose, notability-gated | Sister project. Wikipedia covers what's notable; warpedia covers everything notable in the war domain plus everything below the notability threshold. War-specific entities and primary-source hosting are warpedia's. |
| **Wikimedia Commons** | Open media library | Warpedia mirrors media to Commons where licensing permits, ensuring permanence beyond warpedia itself. |
| **Wikisource** | Source-text transcriptions | Warpedia's transcribed primary sources flow upstream to Wikisource where appropriate. |
| **Wikidata** | Structured entity graph | Warpedia uses Wikidata QIDs as the entity backbone and contributes new war-specific entities upstream. |
| **National archives (NARA, TNA, IWM, AWM, LoC)** | Authoritative source repositories | Warpedia harvests open content, links to closed content, and contextualises both with cross-references and encyclopedic framing. |
| **Genealogy aggregators (Ancestry, Fold3, Findmypast)** | Paid family-history services | Different audience, different purpose. Link out only — no overlap of intent. |
| **Topic-specific archives (Avialogs, HyperWar, Densho, USC Shoah, etc.)** | Deep verticals | Partner, mirror with metadata, or link-out depending on scope and rights. |
| **Memorial / tribute sites (Find a Grave, Ever Loved)** | Commemoration / personal tributes | No overlap. Warpedia includes commemorative *facts* (a name on a memorial, a grave reference) but is not a tributes platform. |
| **War-themed wikis (Military Wiki on Fandom)** | Hobbyist encyclopedic content | Not a peer — Fandom wikis are ad-supported and lower-quality. Warpedia aims for Wikipedia-grade. |

## Notability and scope

Warpedia has **two thresholds**:

| Content type | Threshold |
|---|---|
| **Primary source items** (letters, diaries, photos, manuals, oral histories, citations, etc.) | **None.** Every authentic primary source about a war is welcome, regardless of "importance". A private soldier's single letter is as worthy of preservation as a general's. |
| **Encyclopedic articles** (curated prose pages on Conflicts, Events, Units, People, Equipment, Themes) | **Verifiability.** A curated encyclopedic article exists when there are at least two independent verifiable sources. Below that threshold, the entity may exist as a stub linking to primary sources, but no curated article is generated. |

This split is intentionally looser than Wikipedia's notability — and intentionally tighter than "anyone can write anything". The primary-source corpus grows freely; the encyclopedic layer grows responsibly.

## Editorial standards

- **Cite everything.** Every encyclopedic claim has at least one citation. Inline-cited, traceable.
- **Neutral framing.** Avoid loaded language. Attribute interpretations ("Historians broadly agree..." rather than "It is clear that...").
- **Disclose AI involvement.** Every AI enrichment is recorded in provenance and visibly flagged in the UI.
- **Living relatives.** Items naming living individuals (or recently-deceased ones) require contributor confirmation that next-of-kin have been consulted where appropriate. Privacy concerns trump completeness.
- **Sensitive material.** War crimes, atrocity content, executions, racial / sexual / religious violence, and similar belong in the historical record but require deliberate editorial process — not exclusion, but care.
- **No advocacy.** Warpedia documents; it does not advocate. No takes on whether a war was just, no judgements on individual leaders beyond what reputable secondary sources establish.
- **No promotion.** No vendor / publisher / restoration-business promotion. Restorers and museums can be credited for digitisations; they cannot use warpedia as a marketing channel.

## Governance

**v1 (founding phase, ~years 1–3):**
- Founder-led editorial control.
- Open contributions accepted via PRs / submission forms with editorial review before publication.
- Public roadmap; transparent decision-making.

**v2 (community phase, year 3+):**
- Editorial board including external scholars and archivists.
- Recognised contributors with merge rights.
- Adopt Wikimedia-style governance norms (talk pages, dispute resolution).

**v3+ (institutional phase):**
- Charitable structure (UK CIO or US 501(c)(3)) or fiscal sponsorship via an existing open-knowledge organisation.
- Independent governance independent of any individual.
- The project survives any contributor.

## What success looks like

- A primary-source corpus that becomes the open default for primary war material across multiple conflicts.
- Encyclopedic content cited by other reputable resources (Wikipedia, academic papers, museum exhibits).
- Active mirroring relationship with Wikimedia (Commons, Wikisource, Wikidata).
- Underrepresented voices materially better represented than in any single existing archive.
- A sustainability footing that does not depend on the founders.

## What success does *not* look like

- Subscription paywalls.
- Advertising.
- Becoming a memorial / tributes service.
- Becoming a genealogy service.
- Becoming a current-conflict OSINT site.
- Becoming a forum for opinion or advocacy.

## Relationship to the rest of these docs

This charter is the anchor. When any other document in `docs/` conflicts with this one, this one wins and the other should be revised. When proposing new features, dimensions, or partnerships, the test is: *does this advance the Wikipedia-for-war positioning, or does it pull the project toward genealogy / memorial / opinion / advocacy?* If the latter, decline.
