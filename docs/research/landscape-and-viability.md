# Warpedia — Landscape & Viability Research

*Captured 2026-04-25. A synthesis of the existing players, the unmet needs, and an honest assessment of whether warpedia.org has room to exist.*

## Executive summary

The space is mature at the institutional level, but the institutional players have built mostly **siloed, nation-specific, war-specific archives** with no encyclopedic connective tissue. **Wikipedia covers war notably and well — but its general-purpose, notability-gated, no-primary-source-hosting structure leaves a real gap.** Warpedia is positioned as the war-specific encyclopedic + primary-source resource that gap calls for: Wikipedia-grade encyclopedic content with primary sources hosted directly, war-specific entity structure (Equipment, Camp, Theme, Trial, Treaty) that Wikipedia's article model can't represent, and zero notability bar for primary sources (every diary is welcome, every soldier's name has a place to live). Designed as a multi-decade open knowledge project aligned with the Wikimedia ecosystem, not a startup.

See [`charter.md`](../charter.md) for the formal positioning.

## Headline findings

- **The flagship crowdsourced project in this exact space already shut down.** IWM's *Lives of the First World War* ran 2014–2019 and is now read-only. The institutional appetite for *running* such a platform indefinitely is low.
- **Massive institutional content already exists**, mostly siloed by nation or war and often paywalled.
- **AI is the new frontier** — IWM × Capgemini × Google Cloud opened 20,000 hours of oral history with natural-language search in 2025; Transkribus has 300+ AI models for handwritten historical text.
- **Wikipedia owns reference-style coverage** — competing on neutral encyclopedic content is futile; complementing it with primary sources is not.
- **Centenary funding has rolled off** — the big crowdsourced wins of 2014–2018 are in maintenance or wound-down mode. There is a vacuum for the next generation.
- **Contemporary conflict documentation is exploding** — Ukraine War Archive, EBU Ukraine Archive, NoW corpus show what real-time citizen-archived war looks like in 2025.

## Existing landscape

### Institutional national archives (free, authoritative, siloed)
- **Imperial War Museums (UK)** — runs the read-only *Lives of the First World War* archive; new AI-searchable 20,000-hour oral history corpus with Capgemini/Google. UK-centric, no longer takes contributions.
- **Library of Congress Veterans History Project (US)** — 121,000+ collections of US veteran audio/video, letters, diaries, photos, memoirs (May 2025). US-only, interview-driven.
- **Australian War Memorial** — 9,500+ private records collections; *Anzac Connections* digitised personal WW1 papers. Australian only; most collections still Reading-Room only.
- **US National Archives (NARA)**, **The National Archives (UK)**, **Bibliothèque nationale de France** — hold the underlying official records. Mostly indexed but rarely contextualised with personal narrative.

### Crowdsourced / community projects (centenary-era, mostly winding down)
- **Lives of the First World War (IWM + Findmypast, 2014–2019)** — closed to contributions.
- **Operation War Diary (Zooniverse + IWM + TNA)** — volunteers transcribed/annotated 1.5M pages of WW1 unit diaries. Phase 1 complete.
- **Letters of 1916 (Maynooth)** — Ireland's first crowdsourced humanities project, ~2,400 letters transcribed by ~1,500 volunteers.
- **Europeana 1914–1918** — 400,000+ items including ~100,000 family-submitted images via "Family History Roadshows" in 20+ European countries.

Pattern: big push around an anniversary, then stasis. Volunteers and grant cycles moved on.

### Genealogy-adjacent (paid, polished, commercial)
- **Fold3 (Ancestry)** — military records + member-created memorial pages with family-uploaded photos/stories.
- **Forces War Records (UK)** — 620M records, paid subscription, mixed Trustpilot reviews.
- **Findmypast / Ancestry / FamilySearch** — overlap with the above; FamilySearch is free.

These platforms own the "find my ancestor's service number" intent. Weak on narrative, context, and cross-conflict synthesis.

### Personal / family memorial platforms
**Ever Loved**, Ancestry Memorials, Fold3 memorial pages, Find a Grave — generic obituary scaffolding. Not war-focused. The hosting-forever problem is well-documented.

### Contemporary conflict documentation (new model)
- **Ukraine War Archive** (Docudays + Infoscope, since March 2022) — citizen testimony, war-crimes-grade methodology.
- **EBU Ukraine Archive** — 30,000+ verified video/audio reports.
- **Narratives of War (NoW) corpus** — 1,500 civilian testimonies (academic dataset).

Probably the most innovative current activity in the space.

### AI-driven entrants (2023–2026)
- **StoryFile** — conversational AI lets museum visitors interview pre-recorded veterans (used at the National WW2 Museum; Vietnam Medal of Honor exhibit opened spring 2025).
- **IWM × Capgemini × Google Cloud** — natural-language search across 20,000 hours of conflict oral histories.
- **Witness to War** — 2,000+ interviews, ~30 new/month.
- **Transkribus** — AI HTR for handwritten letters/diaries; the German Archives for Diaries (27,000 diaries, 5,500 authors) is being processed via this approach.

### Wikis
- **Wikipedia WikiProject Military History** — 1,564 FAs, 5,840 GAs. The dominant reference.
- **Military Wiki on Fandom** — exists but much smaller, ad-supported, lower quality.

## Viability assessment

### Reasons for cautious optimism
1. **No existing resource sits in the warpedia gap.** Wikipedia is general-purpose and notability-gated; Wikimedia Commons is media-only; the institutional archives (IWM, AWM, NARA, LoC) are siloed and don't synthesise; the genealogy aggregators are paid and family-tree-focused. A war-specific, primary-source-hosting, encyclopedic, open-licensed resource doesn't exist.
2. **AI tooling has collapsed the cost** — small teams can do what required a research team five years ago.
3. **Wikimedia ecosystem alignment is a sustainability win.** Open licensing + Wikimedia mirroring (Commons, Wikisource, Wikidata) means the work outlives any individual contributor — the natural durability path for an enduring open knowledge project.
4. **Cross-conflict, cross-national integration is genuinely missing.** Every major archive is siloed by nation or war; warpedia's domain-specific entity structure (Equipment, Theme) makes the cross-cutting cuts that institutions structurally can't.

### Real risks
1. **Sustainability is what kills these projects.** Even IWM, with permanent state funding, ended *Lives of the First World War* after five years. Mitigation: Wikimedia mirroring from day one means the content survives even if warpedia itself doesn't.
2. **Wikipedia is both partner and competitor.** Aligning with Wikimedia (open licenses, Commons mirroring, Wikidata reconciliation) is essential — but warpedia must clearly add value beyond what a Wikipedia article provides, or readers won't bother. The hosted-primary-sources + war-specific-entities + sub-notability-coverage combination is the value proposition; if it doesn't materially differentiate, the project loses purpose.
3. **Rights/copyright is the unglamorous hard problem.** Family-submitted content needs clear licensing; archive content has third-party rights; letters can have living-relative privacy concerns.
4. **Verification & moderation never end.** Crowdsourced content invites errors and occasionally bad actors (denial, revisionism, fake submissions). Wikipedia-grade editorial process needed from day one — neutrality, citations, attribution.
5. **Editorial bandwidth scales linearly while content grows exponentially.** Wikipedia took years to build the contributor and admin community that polices it. Warpedia v1 is two people; the editorial-board-and-recognised-contributors transition (charter governance v2) is critical to get right and easy to delay too long.
6. **Volunteer fatigue is real.** The community that transcribed 1.5M Operation War Diary pages did so *because of* the WW1 centenary. Recruiting a sustained contributor base outside such moments is harder.

**Net read:** Viable as a Wikipedia-for-war — open, encyclopedic, primary-source-rich, multi-conflict, federated with the broader Wikimedia ecosystem. The "father-son founding team" frame works at the v1 governance level (small editorial team, personal investment) but the project itself aims at a much wider, sustained ambition. The risk profile is closer to a long-running open-knowledge project than a startup.

## Differentiation angles, ranked by defensibility

| Angle | Defensibility | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Encyclopedic content + hosted primary sources, war-specific | **High** | Wikipedia *cites* primary sources but doesn't *host* them; warpedia does both. The combination is genuinely missing in the field. |
| Domain-specific entity structure (Equipment, Camp, Theme, Trial, Cemetery) | High | Wikipedia's article-per-topic structure can't natively represent these — they exist as scattered articles, not first-class entities. |
| Sub-notability primary-source preservation | High | Wikipedia rejects the private soldier's letter as "non-notable"; warpedia preserves it. Every authentic primary source has a home. |
| Cross-conflict comparative themes (`Theme` entity) | High | Wikipedia structurally can't do "evolution of infantry tactics 1914–2024" — article-per-topic precludes it. |
| Cross-conflict, cross-national personal narratives | High | Institutional archives are siloed by nation/war; warpedia is structurally cross-cutting. |
| AI-assisted contribution (auto-transcribe diaries, OCR old photos, geolocate, link unit/battle) | High | Lowers the bar from "I have a shoebox" to "I have a published item" in minutes. |
| Civilian, women's, indigenous, colonial, minority experiences as first-class | High | Big underserved area; structural editorial commitment is the differentiator, not new schema. |
| Lesser-covered conflicts (Korea, Malayan Emergency, Falklands, Yugoslav wars, African civil wars, peacekeeping) | High | Genuinely thin coverage outside academia. |
| Donate-to-Wikimedia pipeline — every open contribution mirrored to Commons / Wikisource / Wikidata | High | Solves the sustainability problem structurally; aligns with rather than competes against Wikimedia. |
| Federated / linked-data approach — link to existing IWM/AWM/LoC records rather than re-host where possible | Medium | Reduces liability and storage; positions warpedia as the connective tissue between archives. |
| Generic battle/war reference articles ungrounded in primary sources or war-specific structure | Low | Wikipedia covers; warpedia adds value only when paired with the hosted-primary-sources + war-entities differentiators above. |
| Memorial / tributes service | Out of scope | Find a Grave / Ever Loved serve this; warpedia includes commemorative facts but is not a tributes platform — see [charter](../charter.md). |
| Genealogy / family-tree service | Out of scope | Ancestry / Fold3 own this; warpedia hosts personal narratives as primary sources, not as family-tree hooks. |

## Practical considerations
- **Standards**: Dublin Core metadata, IIIF for image presentation, schema.org markup. Don't invent your own schema.
- **Permanence strategy**: Mirror everything to Internet Archive and Wikimedia Commons from day one.
- **Tech stack**: Static-site approach (Hugo/Astro) over a flat-file content store + a small contribution app is more durable than a CMS that needs maintenance.
- **Partnerships**: Local RSL/Legion branches, regimental associations, family-history societies — they have content and need a digital home.
- **Sustainability model**: Charitable structure (UK CIO or US 501(c)(3)) unlocks small grants; fiscal sponsorship via Internet Archive or a digital humanities org is an alternative. Avoid Patreon/membership — audience too small.
- **AI use**: Be transparent — flag AI-generated transcriptions as "machine-assisted, awaiting human review" so trust isn't burnt by errors.

## Open questions to resolve before building
1. Talk to a former *Lives of the First World War* team member — their post-mortem is the most relevant intelligence available.
2. Audit Wikidata's coverage of WW1/WW2 individuals — there may already be a structured-data backbone to build on.
3. Probe the active genealogy/military communities (r/Genealogy, regimental Facebook groups) for what they wish existed.
4. Pilot partnership with one regional archive or museum before committing to architecture.
